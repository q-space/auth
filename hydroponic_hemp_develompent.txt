1. Legal & Compliance Foundations
Item	What to Do	Why It Matters
Licensing	Obtain the required cultivation license from your national or regional authority (e.g., USDA‑APHIS in the U.S., Health Canada, EU member‑state regulator).	Without a proper license you cannot legally produce THC‑restricted crops.
THC Limits	Verify the maximum allowable Δ⁹‑THC concentration for “industrial/hemp” in your jurisdiction (commonly ≤ 0.3 % dry weight in the U.S. and many EU countries).	Exceeding limits can reclassify the crop as marijuana, triggering stricter regulations.
Seed Certification	Purchase certified low‑THC seed varieties from approved suppliers. Many regulators require a certificate of analysis (COA) confirming genetics.	Guarantees you start with a compliant genotype and helps traceability.
Record‑Keeping	Implement a digital log (e.g., Lumo Plus can store encrypted notes) tracking seed batch, nutrient regimes, harvest dates, and lab test results.	Required for audits and for ensuring product consistency.
Tip: Before you begin, consult your local agricultural extension office or a licensed attorney familiar with cannabis law to confirm all licensing steps.

2. Hydroponic System Selection
System Type	Pros	Cons	Typical Use Cases
Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)	Simple, low water use, fast growth	Sensitive to pump failure; root clogging possible	Small‑scale, leafy growth phases
Deep Water Culture (DWC)	Large oxygenated root zone, easy to monitor pH/EC	Requires robust aeration; can lead to rapid nutrient uptake (risk of burn)	Mid‑stage vegetative growth
Ebb & Flow (Flood & Drain)	Good for larger plants, flexible media	More components, timing critical	Full life cycle from veg to flower
Aeroponics	Highest oxygen exposure, fastest growth rates	Complex, higher maintenance, expensive	Research or premium‑quality yields
Recommendation for beginners: Start with a DWC or Ebb & Flow system. Both give you ample control over nutrient delivery while being forgiving enough for early learning curves.

3. Environmental Parameters
Parameter	Target Range (vegetative)	Target Range (flowering)	How to Control
Temperature	20–26 °C (68–79 °F)	18–24 °C (64–75 °F)	HVAC, heat mats, exhaust fans
Relative Humidity	55–70 %	40–55 %	Humidifier/dehumidifier, ventilation
CO₂	Ambient (≈ 400 ppm) – optional boost to 800–1200 ppm	Same as veg, but watch for heat	CO₂ tank + regulator, monitoring sensor
Light Spectrum	400–700 nm, ~300–600 µmol·m⁻²·s⁻¹ PPFD	600–900 µmol·m⁻²·s⁻¹ PPFD, more red (660 nm)	Full‑spectrum LEDs, adjustable dimmers
Photoperiod	18 h light / 6 h dark	12 h light / 12 h dark (or 10 h for faster flowering)	Timer-controlled lighting
Note: Maintaining tight humidity control during flowering reduces mold risk—a common issue in dense canopy crops like hemp.

4. Nutrient Management
Base Nutrient Formula

Vegetative Phase: Higher N (nitrogen) – e.g., NPK 3‑1‑2.
Flowering Phase: Lower N, higher P & K – e.g., NPK 1‑3‑4.
Micronutrients

Calcium, magnesium, iron, zinc, copper, manganese, boron, molybdenum.
Use a chelated micronutrient mix to prevent precipitation in recirculating systems.
Electrical Conductivity (EC) / TDS

Veg: 1.2–1.8 mS/cm (800–1400 ppm).
Flower: 1.8–2.4 mS/cm (1400–1900 ppm).
pH Range

Keep reservoir pH between 5.8–6.2. Use pH up/down solutions and a calibrated meter.
Feeding Schedule

Daily: Check EC and pH, adjust as needed.
Weekly: Flush the system with plain water (pH 6.0) for 30 min to prevent salt buildup.
Safety Reminder: Wear gloves and eye protection when handling concentrated nutrient solutions.

5. Plant Training & Canopy Management
Technique	Purpose	Implementation Tips
Topping/FIM	Promote lateral growth, increase bud sites	Perform early (3–5 nodes), keep topmost growth.
Low Stress Training (LST)	Spread canopy, improve light penetration	Bend stems gently, secure with soft ties.
Screen of Green (ScrOG)	Uniform canopy, maximize yield per square foot	Install a mesh screen at 30 cm height; weave branches through as they grow.
Defoliation (moderate)	Reduce shading, improve airflow	Remove lower fan leaves once the plant reaches ~50 cm height.
6. Harvest, Drying & Curing
Harvest Timing

Monitor trichome color with a handheld microscope: clear → milky → amber. For medical hemp, many growers stop at 70–80 % milky to preserve cannabinoids while keeping THC low.
Drying

Hang trimmed buds in a dark, well‑ventilated room at 15–18 °C (59–64 °F) and 45–55 % RH for 7–10 days.
Curing

Transfer dried buds to airtight glass jars, open (“burp”) daily for the first week, then weekly thereafter. Cure for at least 2–4 weeks before lab testing.
Testing

Send samples to an accredited lab for cannabinoid profile, pesticide residues, and moisture content. Use the results to verify compliance with THC limits.
7. Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them
Issue	Symptoms	Prevention
Nutrient Burn	Yellowing leaf tips, crispy edges	Keep EC within recommended range; flush regularly.
Root Rot (Pythium)	Soft, brown roots, foul smell	Ensure proper aeration, use UV‑sterilized water, monitor temperature (< 22 °C).
Powdery Mildew	White powder on leaves/buds	Maintain low humidity during flowering, improve airflow, consider sulfur‑based fungicide (if permitted).
THC Over‑limit	Lab results > 0.3 %	Use certified low‑THC seeds, monitor stress (light intensity, temperature) that can raise THC production.
